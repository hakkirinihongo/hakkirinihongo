What exactly is the Japanese auxiliary 込む（こむ）, and what meaning does it really add in compound verbs that contain it?

The auxiliary 込む (*komu*) can originally be understood as an element built on the **concrete idea of “going into”** or **“putting something inside.”** In compound verbs, however, this original image extends far beyond a physical meaning and can express entry, depth, intensity, involvement, or even the completion of an action.

In its most transparent uses, it indicates **movement inward**, as in 飛び込む (*“to jump / dive into”*) or 書き込む (*“to write into / enter”*). In more grammaticalized uses, however, it brings to the foreground **the full arrival into a state, remaining in that state**, intensification, carrying out an action thoroughly or repeatedly, and even mental absorption, as in 座り込む, 黙り込む, 冷え込む, 考え込む, and 思い込む.

For this reason, Japanese teaching materials often refer to it as an *“auxiliary,”* but in Japanese linguistics it is very commonly analyzed as a **後項動詞**, that is, the *“second verb”* in a compound verb structure of the type **V1 + V2**.

From a historical perspective, the lexical field of 込む revolves around the ideas of *enclosure, inclusion, filling,* and *containment.* Historical dictionaries connect the modern area of 込む with classical forms such as 込める / 籠める and 籠もる, and show that the concrete sense of *“putting something inside / being enclosed”* is ancient. From there also developed meanings such as *“being dense / crowded,”* *“being elaborated,”* and eventually the modern compound-verb uses.

Today the formation rule is straightforward: take the **-ます stem** of the verb and add **込む**; the entire compound then conjugates as a **godan verb ending in -む.**

Its use is extremely broad: everyday speech, fiction, journalism, technical writing, and even literary prose.

## 込む (komu) – meaning and function

As we have already seen, the clearest semantic core of **-込む** is the idea of **movement inward**.

Japanese dictionaries describe it both intransitively as **“to go inside”** and transitively as **“to put something inside.”**

That is why:

**飛び込む** means *“to jump into,”*

**流れ込む** means *“to flow into,”*

while **書き込む**, **詰め込む**, and **呼び込む** mean *“to write / insert into,”* *“to pack tightly into,”* and *“to bring in / draw inside.”*

This is the basic spatial meaning, and it is often accompanied by a destination marked with **に.**


From this core develops a second very common value, which we could describe as **settling into a state.**

Here **-込む** does not simply indicate that an action has chronologically finished.

Rather, it signals that the subject *“enters”* a state and then remains there.

So:

**座り込む** is not simply *“to sit down,”* but **“to sit down and stay there.”**

**黙り込む** means **“to fall into a persistent silence.”**

**寝込む** means **“to take to bed / end up bedridden.”**

**泊まり込む** means **“to stay overnight somewhere and remain there.”**

In other words, the nuance of **completion** here is primarily that of a **solidified resulting state**, not simply *“finishing.”*


A third value is **intensification, deepening, or carrying something out thoroughly.**

Japanese dictionaries describe this as *“doing something completely, deeply, excessively, or for a long time,”* and the NINJAL lexical database (*National Institute for Japanese Language and Linguistics*) also connects **-込む** with repetition, habit, and reinforcement of a result.

That is why:

**煮込む** means **“to simmer for a long time.”**

**使い込む** means **“to use repeatedly until one becomes familiar with it or wears it down.”**

**読み込む** can mean **“to read deeply / load data.”**

**走り込む** may mean **“to train intensively by running.”**

And **冷え込む** does not indicate merely *“getting cold,”* but rather **a marked and penetrating drop in temperature.**


A fourth value concerns **mental and psychological states.**

The *日本国語大辞典* (*Nihon Kokugo Daijiten* – *Comprehensive Dictionary of the Japanese Language*, Shogakukan) explicitly distinguishes a use in which the mind *“closes itself off”* and no longer takes anything else in.

This is what happens in verbs such as:

**考え込む** and **ふさぎ込む.**

Likewise, with attitudes or convictions such as:

**思い込む** or **信じ込む,**

the compound tends to portray the subject as deeply absorbed in their own internal view of things.

In this group the nuance is often stronger and less neutral than in the base verb.

For example:

**思う** = *“to think”*  but **思い込む** = *“to become firmly convinced,”* often in a rigid, one-sided, or mistaken way.


A fifth value, especially useful when translating into English, is that of **involvement or immersion** in an activity, a social setting, or even a *“psychological space.”*

Recent linguistic studies often explain this development through a **container metaphor**:

physical space becomes social or mental space.

This makes uses such as:

**打ち込む** (*“to devote oneself completely”*)

very easy to understand.

Likewise, people, ideas, or content can be *“brought into”* a system, a group, a text, or a practice.

Depending on the context, **-込む** may therefore be rendered in English as:

*“into,”*  
*“deeply,”*  
*“all the way,”*  
*“to immerse oneself,”*  
*“to become absorbed,”*  
*“to involve,”*  
or *“to drill into / impress firmly.”*

## Historical origin and development

The word is native Japanese, while the character **込** itself is a **kokuji**, that is, a kanji created in Japan.

The *デジタル大辞泉* (*Daijisen*) explicitly notes this.

This matters because it reminds us that the semantic core of **komu** does not come from a Sino-Japanese reading of the character, but from an older Japanese lexical family later represented with several spellings, especially **込** and **籠.**


Historically, dictionaries show a whole cluster of closely related forms.

**込める / 籠める** is recorded as a modern verb continuing the classical **こむ** of the **下二段** type, with meanings such as:

*“to put inside,”*  
*“to enclose,”*  
*“to cover from outside,”*  
*“to hide,”*  
*“to include,”*  
*“to load with feeling.”*

**籠もる** is presented as the intransitive counterpart:

*“to be enclosed,”*  
*“to remain shut inside,”*  
*“to contain,”*  
*“to have something within.”*

This historical pair makes the semantic shift very plausible:

from *“contain / enclose”* to more abstract modern values such as

*“to internalize,”*  
*“to concentrate,”*  
*“to become fixed on,”*  
or *“to remain trapped in a state.”*


Modern **込む** used independently also has ancient roots.

The *日本国語大辞典* documents the meaning *“to be crowded / full”* already in the **紫式部日記** around the year 1010, and later texts show the sense of *“being elaborate / intricate.”*

So long before contemporary Japanese, the semantic field already included the ideas of density, concentration, and complexity.

It is easy to see the thread connecting:

*“many things inside one space,”*  
*“parts tightly woven together,”*  
and *“a state that deepens or becomes firmly established.”*


The semantic development toward figurative uses is often explained through the **container metaphor.**

A recent study on **V1 + 入れる / 込む / 詰める** illustrates very clearly the transition

**from physical space to social and psychological space.**

What is *“inside”* may be inside a container, a group, the mind, or the heart.

Within this framework, **-込む** keeps its fundamental trait of interiority, but applies it less and less to physical interiors and more and more to cognitive, social, and textual ones.

This is why:

**叩き込む**

can mean both *“to drive into”* and *“to drill firmly into someone’s mind,”*

and

**打ち込む**

can move from a physical action to **total immersion in an activity.**

## Grammatical structure and formation rules

The general rule is:

**-masu stem + 込む**

The grammar materials of TUFS (*Tokyo University of Foreign Studies*) define the **連用形** precisely as the form obtained by removing **ます** from the **ます-form**.

So:

読む → 読み  
書く → 書き  
飛ぶ → 飛び  
考える → 考え  
寝る → 寝

and so on;

then **込む** is added to that stem.


For the main verb groups, the concrete formation works as follows:

with **-u verbs**, the verb shifts to the **-i stem**

(読む → 読み込む, 書く → 書き込む, 飛ぶ → 飛び込む);

with **ichidan -ru verbs**, **-る** is removed

(考える → 考え込む, 寝る → 寝込む);

with irregular verbs, the same logic as the **ます-form** applies,

so theoretically

する → し-  
来る → き-

However, in the sources consulted, the **-込む** pattern is discussed mainly with ordinary lexical verbs.

With **する** and **来る**, it does not emerge as a particularly productive pattern.

Because of that, any lexicalized forms involving those verbs are best learned individually, rather than treated as freely combinable.


Once formed, the compound conjugates like a normal **godan verb ending in -む**:

書き込む → 書き込んだ → 書き込んで → 書き込めば

The same applies to:

考え込む  
冷え込む  
住み込む

This is because the final element is **込む**, which dictionaries classify as a **マ五** verb (a godan verb whose consonant row ends in **m**).


From a structural point of view, the *National Institute for Japanese Language and Linguistics* often distinguishes two major readings in compound verbs with **込む.**


In the **VV reading** (a lexical compound verb, where 込む still retains much of its full verbal meaning), **込む preserves a fairly concrete sense**, close to *“to go inside,”* *“to enter,”* or *“to move inward.”*

In these cases, the first verb expresses the main action, while 込む still clearly retains the idea of interiority or actual entry.

In expressions such as:

**部屋に入り込む**  
or  
**交番に走り込む**

the movement toward a concrete place is directly visible:

someone really enters a space,

and the original meaning of 込む remains clearly perceptible.


In the **Vs reading** (a more grammaticalized compound, where 込む functions almost like an auxiliary and loses part of the concrete sense of *“going inside”*), **the spatial meaning becomes less central**, and 込む instead adds nuances such as intensity, depth, completion, persistence, or deep involvement.

In **考え込む**, for example, there is no physical entry at all:

the verb suggests *immersing oneself in thought until one becomes absorbed by it.*

The same applies to:

**信じ込む**, meaning *“to become deeply convinced of something,”*

(similar to **決め込む**, *“to settle firmly on something, even if that decision may be mistaken”*)

or to

**読み込む**, where the meaning tends toward *“to read thoroughly”* or *“to absorb completely.”*

Likewise:

**試合前に走り込む**

does not mean *“to run into a place,”*

but rather *“to train intensively through running until one’s preparation becomes solid.”*


As a practical guideline:

when a concrete location appears with **に**, and the context clearly suggests entering or moving inward, the more literal reading is often the most natural.

When no destination is present, and the meaning revolves mainly around intensity, result, or deep involvement, 込む tends to be perceived in a more grammaticalized way.

Naturally, the boundary is not rigid.

And that is precisely one of the most interesting features of this structure:

the same verb can shift between a concrete and figurative value depending on context,

and often it is the sentence as a whole that determines which nuance becomes strongest.

## Register, usage domains, and restrictions

Verbs in **-込む** **do not belong only to casual spoken Japanese.**

Studies on compound verbs consistently note that these forms appear **across everyday conversation, fiction, essays, technical writing, and even academic prose.**

This matters because it avoids a common mistake:

**-込む is not merely a “spoken-language coloring.”**

Some compounds are colloquial or emotionally marked,

but the pattern as a whole is central to standard Japanese vocabulary.


That said, internal distribution is not uniform.

Forms such as:

**話し込む**  
**黙り込む**  
**ふさぎ込む**  
**思い込む**

are extremely natural in conversation, fiction, and descriptions of internal states.

Others, such as:

**取り込む**  
**組み込む**  
**盛り込む**  
**読み込む**

are also strongly present in technical, administrative, or academic registers.

So **-込む itself is neither colloquial nor formal.**

What determines register is the individual lexical item that has become established in usage.


A crucial point is **lexical restriction.**

NINJAL notes that second verbs with a more auxiliary-like function combine only with a limited set of first verbs.

The teaching materials of **姫野伴香** (*Himeno Tomoka*) illustrate this with a classic question:

*“Why can we say 駆け込む but not 歩き込む?”*

In other words:

**knowing the rule “-masu stem + 込む” is not enough.**

One must also know **which combinations are actually alive in real usage, and with what meaning.**

That is why many compounds are best learned as individual lexical units.


**Collocations** also help clarify meaning.

With inward-motion uses, a destination marked by **に** is common,

and the sense of interiority becomes even stronger with expressions such as:

**～の中に**  
or  
**～の奥に**

With mental-state uses, on the other hand, verbs such as:

**思い込む**  
**信じ込む**  
**決め込む**

often occur with quoted content introduced by **と**,

and may appear alongside adverbs such as:

**勝手に**  
**てっきり**

or other elements that emphasize one-sidedness, error, or a mismatch with reality.

In short:

**-込む does not merely carry a broad abstract meaning; it interacts strongly with the nouns and adverbs around it.**


It is also worth remembering that **some forms now function almost like simple lexical words**, and are no longer fully transparent as V1 + 込む.

Studies often cite cases such as:

**めり込む**  
**見込む**  
**振り込む**

and note that even forms such as **読み込む** may behave, at least partly, like already lexicalized units.

From a practical point of view, the best strategy is therefore twofold:

**understand the general semantic core of -込む,**

but also

**learn the most frequent or idiomatic compounds individually.**


Finally, there is no clear dialectal restriction on the pattern.

**-込む** belongs firmly to contemporary standard Japanese.

Visible differences mainly concern the choice of individual compounds, their degree of lexicalization, and sometimes spelling

(**込む / こむ / 混む**),

rather than any true regional distribution of the pattern itself.

## Conclusion

In summary, **込む is one of the richest and most subtle mechanisms in Japanese verbal expression.**

Its semantic center is the idea of **“inside”**:

**to go inside, put inside, make something penetrate inward.**

From that center develop the meanings of settling into a state, deepening, intensification, accumulation, involvement, and mental closure.

Historically, it belongs to an old lexical family related to **込める** and **籠もる.**

Grammatically, it is formed by attaching to the **-masu stem** of a verb.

Lexically, however, it is not completely free, because many compounds are selective or already lexicalized.

For learners of Japanese, the safest rule is this:

**start from the image of “inside,”**

then ask yourself whether that *“inside”* is

**physical, psychological, social, textual, or aspectual.**

It is precisely this continuity between concrete space and abstract state that makes **-込む** so productive—

and at the same time so challenging to truly master.
